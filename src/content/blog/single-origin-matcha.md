Matcha sounds like one simple thing, but what's actually inside the tin varies a lot depending on the brand. Today, we're breaking down a concept that's genuinely useful to understand before buying matcha: single-origin.

## The Reality: Most Matcha Is Blended

Most of the matcha on the market today is actually a blend, made from tea leaves sourced from multiple regions and farms. That's not a bad thing on its own. Blending has real, practical advantages: it keeps flavor consistent, helps control costs, and ensures the same quality year after year. Most matcha brands blend for exactly these reasons, and it's a completely legitimate approach.

That said, blended matcha does come with a trade-off: it becomes harder to know exactly where the tea came from, or what makes that particular region's tea unique.

## So What Is Single-Origin Matcha?

Single-origin matcha is matcha made entirely from tea leaves grown in one specific region, often from a single tea farm.

If you've heard the term "single-origin coffee" before, the concept translates almost directly. Instead of blending for consistency, single-origin matcha lets one region's character shine through, unfiltered.

Here's what tends to set single-origin matcha apart:

- **A distinct sense of place**: Climate, soil, and growing methods all show up directly in the flavor and aroma.
- **Some year-to-year variation**: This isn't a sign of inconsistent quality. It simply reflects that year's weather and harvest conditions, much like wine vintages.
- **A clearer connection to the maker**: It's easier to know exactly which farm grew your tea, and the philosophy behind how it's made.

## Why Single-Origin Matters

Choosing single-origin matcha is a bit like appreciating terroir in wine, that idea that a specific place leaves its fingerprint on the final product.

Depending on where the matcha comes from, you'll notice real differences in:

- Sweetness and umami intensity
- The balance of bitterness
- How the aroma develops
- Color

None of this means blended matcha is inferior. It's simply a different experience, one built for consistency rather than showcasing a single region's character.

## What to Look For

When shopping for matcha, check the packaging for terms like "single-origin," a specific prefecture, or a named tea farm. If none of that information is listed, there's a good chance you're looking at a blend.

Neither option is inherently "better." It really comes down to what kind of experience you're looking for.
